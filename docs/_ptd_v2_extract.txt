1. Introduction 
1.1 Problem Statement 
Buying and selling second-hand, surplus, and collectible goods online is dominated by fixed-price 
marketplaces, where the seller alone sets the price and market demand is not allowed to discover 
a fair value. Where auction mechanisms do exist, they are oft en mixed into big, convoluted 
platforms, they might not show a clear bidding timeline, or provide weak trust and safety features 
such as user ratings, reporting, and moderation. Many tools also handle personal data carelessly, 
which is a growing concern under data-protection regulation. There is a need for a focused, secure, 
and easy-to-use web platform on which sellers can list items and buyers can compete through 
real-time bidding, supported by reputation, moderation, and privacy-aware data handling. 
1.2 Purpose 
We aim to deliver a trustworthy and accessible online auction platform where anyone can buy 
and sell goods through fair, transparent, real-time bidding. By combining multiple auction formats, 
proxy bidding, reputation and reporting features, and strong, pr ivacy-by-design security, the 
platform helps buyers and sellers transact with confidence. 
1.3 Objectives 
• To provide secure authentication and account management, including two -factor 
authentication (TOTP) and OTP-based password reset. 
• To support multiple auction formats — standard price-up (English), Dutch, and blind/sealed 
auctions. 
• To enable real-time competitive bidding with transactional integrity and automated proxy 
(auto-) bidding. 
• To make discovery easy through keyword search, multi -attribute filtering, sorting, and 
personalised recommendations. 
• To build trust through public bid histories, seller profiles, bi -directional ratings, an auction 
Q&A, and abuse reporting. 
• To give administrators tools to moderate users, listings, and categories, and to view 
platform analytics and reports. 
• To protect personal data in line with Singapore's PDPA through encryption at rest, masking, 
minimal collection, and account-deletion anonymisation. 
• To deliver a responsive, browser-based interface and a tested, maintainable MVC + DAO 
codebase.

2.2 Unique Selling Point 
AuctionHub differentiates itself by combining transparent, multi -format real -time auctions with 
strong, built-in privacy and trust features. 
2.2.1 Multiple Auction Formats 
Sellers can run standard price -up (English), Dutch (declining -price), or blind (sealed -bid) 
auctions, choosing the format that best suits the item. 
2.2.2 Proxy / Automatic Bidding 
Buyers can set a maximum bid, and the system bids on their behalf up to that ceiling. The 
ceiling and any note are encrypted at rest, and proxy counter -bids are resolved atomically 
within the same transaction as the triggering bid. 
2.2.3 Privacy by Design 
Personal data (phone, address), the 2FA secret, and auto-bid ceilings are encrypted with AES-
256-GCM; passwords are salted -hashed; public displays use masking; and account deletion 
anonymises personal data in line with PDPA. 
2.2.4 Transparency and Trust 
Public bid histories, public seller profiles, bi -directional ratings, an auction Q&A, and admin 
moderation of users, listings, and categories give participants the information and safeguards 
they need to transact with confidence.

2.3 Target Users 
The platform supports distinct user categories, each with its own access level. A registered 
account holds a single role (Buyer, Seller, or Admin). 
2.3.1 Guest / Unregistered User 
• Browses the landing page, searches and filters listings, and views auction details, bid 
history, and public seller profiles. 
• Cannot bid, list items, or use account features; is prompted to register to unlock them. 
2.3.2 Buyer 
• Searches, watches, and bids on auctions, including setting auto-bids. 
• Asks questions, rates sellers, reports abuse, and manages their own account. 
2.3.3 Seller 
• Creates, edits, and cancels auction listings and manages them from a dashboard. 
• Replies to buyer questions, rates buyers, maintains a public profile, and manages their 
account. 
2.3.4 System Admin 
• Moderates users (ban / unban), listings (flag / remove / restore), and categories (CRUD). 
• Views platform metrics, analytics, and generated reports. 
2.4 Business Model 
AuctionHub operates as a C2C online auction marketplace focused on second-
hand and collectible goods in Singapore and Southeast Asia. The prototype implements core m
arketplace flows (listing, bidding, simulated escrow, ratings, moderation) and two concrete reven
ue mechanisms recorded in the platform_revenue table. Payment gateways and logistics integr
ations described below are planned partners for a production launch; the current system uses si
mulated escrow and does not process real payments.

8. Software Methodology 
 
Source: https://www.scrum.org/resources/scrum-framework-poster 
We are using Scrum as our development methodology, supported by Jira as our project 
management tool. This project requires multiple features to be developed and will undergo some 
changes based on supervisor feedback, which makes an iterative approach more appropriate than 
trying to plan everything upfront. Jira lets us manage this iterative workflow in one place, giving 
the whole team visibility into what is being worked on and what is coming next. 
1. Our team utilizes the Scrum framework to help us develop and deliver the product efficiently 
and on time. Scrum provides a structured and iterative approach that enables the team to 
manage tasks effectively, adapt to changes, and continuously improve throu ghout the 
project lifecycle. We use Jira to operationalise this framework, maintaining our backlog, 
boards, and sprints in a single shared workspace.

2. We conduct biweekly sprint planning sessions where the team defines sprint goals, 
identifies priorities, and determines realistic, achievable objectives for the upcoming sprint. 
During planning, we create a Jira sprint and pull the highest -priority user stories from the 
product backlog into the sprint backlog, assigning owners and estimates so that the 
committed scope is clear and trackable. 
3. We conduct a sprint review at the end to evaluate the work completed and identify any 
obstacles or challenges encountered during development. This provides an opportunity for 
the team to discuss areas for improvement and reflect on the effectiveness of the  sprint 
process. Jira's board and sprint reports (such as the burndown chart and completed-versus-

4. We refine the backlog with feedback from the sprint review and update it accordingly in Jira.

ensure that future sprints are aligned with the overall project goals an d stakeholder 
expectations. Carried-over and newly raised issues are re-ranked in the backlog so the next

• Sprint Backlog: the specific issues and sub-tasks we've committed to for the current sprint,

• Increment: the working software we deliver at the end of each sprint, corresponding to the

The reason Scrum works well with this project is that building an auction platform involves a lot of 
interconnected features such as the auction engine, the recommendation system, payment flow, 
and admin tools. We also need the flexibility to adjust priori ties as we go. Two-week sprints give 
us enough time to build something meaningful while still receiving feedback from the supervisor, 
and Jira keeps these moving parts organised by linking related issues, tracking progress in real 
time, and making dependencies between features visible to the whole team. 
9. Technical stack 
9.1 Frontend 
The primary user interface is a React 19 single-page application (SPA) in FYP/Frontend, built with 
Vite 8, styled with Tailwind CSS 3, and routed with React Router 7. The client calls the backend 
through Axios against /api/*, sending a per -tab Bearer token  from sessionStorage in the 
Authorization header (withCredentials is enabled for cookie -backed flows where needed). 
Production builds use VITE_BASE=/online -auction/ so static assets resolve under the Tomcat

context path. Key UI libraries include lucide -react (icons) and qrcode.react (2FA setup). In local 
development, the Vite dev server runs on port 3000 and proxies /api and /uploads to Tomcat at 
http://localhost:8080/online-auction. React was chosen for comp onent reuse, fast iteration, and 
interactive screens such as live bidding (SSE-driven price updates), seller dashboards, and admin 
tools. A smaller set of legacy JSP/JSTL pages styled with Bootstrap 5.3 remains for backward 
compatibility; all new features are implemented in React. 
9.2 Backend 
The server is Java 11, packaged as a WAR (online-auction.war) and deployed on Apache Tomcat 
10.1+ (Jakarta Servlet 6.0). The application follows MVC + DAO: *ApiServlet classes under 
com.auction.servlet.api extend ApiBase, handle HTTP routing and validation , authenticate via 
TokenStore / AuthSession (Bearer tokens for the React client), and return JSON serialised with 
Jackson 2.17. Persistence is isolated in com.auction.dao using JDBC and PreparedStatement 
only; servlets do not embed SQL. Cross-cutting Filters run before servlets: SecurityFilter (security 
headers on all requests), CorsFilter (cross-origin API access in development), SpaFallbackFilter 
(serves the React index.html for client -side routes), plus AuthFilter and AdminFilter on legacy 
/protected/* a nd /admin/* paths. Real -time bid updates use Server -Sent Events (SSE) via 
AuctionEventServlet at GET /api/auction -events/{auctionId}, triggered after successful bids by 
AuctionEventPublisher. Legacy servlet + JSP controllers under com.auction.servlet are r etained 
for regression coverage; the React SPA is the primary presentation path. Jakarta Servlets were 
chosen as a lightweight, well -understood server model that fits transactional auction logic and 
integrates cleanly with PostgreSQL through JDBC. 
9.3 Database 
PostgreSQL is the relational database, accessed via the PostgreSQL JDBC driver 42.7.7 and 
HikariCP 7.0.2 connection pooling (DBUtil). Schema and migrations live under 
FYP/src/main/resources/ (e.g. auction_db.sql, migrate_all.sql). PostgreSQL was selected f or 
ACID transactions (essential for row -locked bidding), referential integrity, indexing, and mature 
tooling. MySQL was the PRD baseline but was replaced for stronger concurrency semantics and 
consistency with the Render managed PostgreSQL offering. SQLite was rejected because it does 
not suit concurrent multi-user web workloads. MongoDB was rejected because auction, bid, and 
order data are highly relational and benefit from SQL joins and SELECT … FOR UPDATE locking 
in BidDAO.placeBid.

13.2 System Architecture Design 
AuctionHub is deployed as a single web application (online-auction) on Apache Tomcat inside a 
Docker container on Render.com. The architecture separates concerns into three tiers 
(presentation, application, data), uses MVC + DAO inside the application tier, pushes live 
auction updates through Server-Sent Events (SSE), and persists data in PostgreSQL with 
optional persistent disk for user-uploaded files.

via SSE (/api/auction-events/{id}); React build artefacts and WAR deployed together on Tomcat

13.2.6 Request lifecycle (typical bid flow) 
This subsection describes the ascending auction (Price Up) place-bid flow as implemented in 
the React + JSON API stack. A full sequence diagram is provided in the project repository 
at docs/sequence-diagrams/ (updated mentally below to match the API path). 
 
Steps 
1. Browse: User opens /auction/{id} in the React SPA. The page loads auction detail 
via GET /api/auction/{id} (or equivalent detail endpoint) and opens an SSE connection 
to /api/auction-events/{id}. 
2. Submit bid: User enters amount; React posts POST 
/api/bid with auctionId and bidAmount (form or application/x-www-form-urlencoded), 
including Bearer token. 
3. Filter chain: SecurityFilter adds headers; CorsFilter applies if cross-origin; request 
reaches BidApiServlet. 
4. Controller:  BidApiServlet validates session (buyer role), parses amounts, rejects self-
bids and invalid input. 
5. DAO transaction: BidDAO.placeBid: 
• SELECT … FOR UPDATE on auction row 
• Validates active status, end time, moderation state, floor price, max price 
• INSERT bid 
• AutoBidDAO.processAutoBids(conn, auctionId) in the same transaction 
• COMMIT 
6. Post-commit: On success: 
• AuctionEventPublisher.publishSnapshot(auctionId) → SSE clients update 
• NotificationService.notifyOutbid(...) → in-app notification for previous high bidder 
• 200 OK JSON message to the bidder’s browser

7. UI update: React shows success; SSE may refresh price before or in addition to the 
POST response handler. 
 
Failure paths 
• Bid too low, auction closed, self-bid, exceeds max price → transaction rolled back 
→ 400 JSON error (no SSE broadcast). 
• Unauthenticated or wrong role → 401 / 403 from ApiBase.

13.6 UML Class Diagram 
 
The diagram below summarises the Model–View–Controller structure of AuctionHub as 
implemented for the prototype demo. 
- View (presentation tier): The primary view is the React 19 SPA (FYP/Frontend), which 
renders pages and components and calls the backend through JSON over HTTP. A 
small set of legacy JSP views remains for backward compatibility; new features are 
implemented in React. 
- Controller (application tier): ApiBase is the abstract parent of all *ApiServlet classes 
under com.auction.servlet.api. Each API servlet handles routing, bearer-token 
authentication (TokenStore / AuthSession), input validation, and JSON 
responses. Filters (SecurityFilter, CorsFilter, SpaFallbackFilter) run before 
servlets. Realtime and notification components 
(AuctionEventPublisher, AuctionEventBus, AuctionEventServlet, NotificationService) act 
as controller-side coordinators after state-changing operations such as a successful bid. 
- Model (domain and data tier): Entity classes in com.auction.model represent users, 
auctions, bids, orders, and related domain concepts. DAO 
classes in com.auction.dao encapsulate all JDBC access to PostgreSQL 
using PreparedStatement and HikariCP (DBUtil). Utilities (SecurityUtil, InputValidator, 
etc.) provide cross-cutting encryption, validation, and masking. 
The controller never embeds SQL; it delegates persistence to DAOs and returns DTO-style 
model objects (e.g. AuctionDetail, SearchResultItem) to the view as JSON.

14.2 Test Strategy 
The team uses a combined strategy appropriate for a servlet-based web application with a 
separate React client. 
 
14.2.1 White-box unit testing (primary) 
• Framework: JUnit 5 (Jupiter 5.10.2) + Mockito. 
• Scope: API servlets (*ApiServlet), legacy servlets (/protected/*, /admin/*), filters, DAO 
orchestration, and pure utility/algorithm code. 
• Technique: Servlets are tested through a thin Wrapper subclass that 
exposes doGet / doPost. DAOs are mocked with Mockito so tests run without a live 
database. JSON API tests use ApiTestSupport for session/bearer binding and response 
capture. 
• Rationale: Fast feedback on business rules, RBAC, validation, and error paths; supports 
regression when migrating from JSP servlets to /api/*. 
 
14.2.2 Black-box manual testing (secondary) 
• Deployed WAR + React bundle on Tomcat (local or Render). 
• Manual scripts cover navigation, forms, flash/JSON messages, live bidding (SSE price 
update), and admin flows not worth automating in Selenium for this FYP scope.

14.2.3 Boundary Value Analysis (BVA) and Equivalence Partitioning (EP) 
Applied to high-risk inputs: 
Domain Boundary / partition examples 
Bidding Zero/negative amount; equal to current highest; one cent above; 
above max_price; self-bid; closed auction 
Ratings Score outside 1–5; duplicate review 
Search Empty keyword; overlong query; invalid minPrice/maxPrice; invalid condition; 
non-whitelist sortBy 
Pagination Page 0, page beyond last page, invalid size 
Auth Missing session; wrong role; suspended account 
 
14.2.4 Security-oriented testing 
• RBAC: Buyer / seller / admin / guest access matrices on servlets and filters 
(TestAdminFilter, TestBidApiServlet, etc.). 
• IDOR prevention: User and role identifiers taken from server session (AuthSession), not 
trusted from client parameters. 
• Injection: SQL injection strings in search filters treated as invalid input or bound 
parameters (no string concatenation in DAOs). 
• Privacy: Email/username masking and AES-256-GCM encryption assertions 
in SecurityUtilTest, profile, bid-history, and 2FA tests. 
• HTTP headers: SecurityFilter CSP and anti-clickjacking headers (TestSecurityFilter). 
Out of scope for this submission: formal penetration testing or third-party white-hat audit. 
Security confidence is from design (prepared statements, filters, encryption) plus unit-level 
tests.

15. Conclusion 
AuctionHub delivers a focused, transparent online auction platform for second -hand and 
collectible goods. By combining multiple auction formats, real-time and proxy bidding, and strong 
trust and privacy features on a tested MVC + DAO architecture, it offers buyers and sellers a fair 
and secure way to transact. The conventional, well-understood technology stack (Jakarta Servlets, 
PostgreSQL, React) keeps the system maintainable and lower -risk, while the modular design 
leaves clear room for future growth such as real payment integration and a mobile application.
